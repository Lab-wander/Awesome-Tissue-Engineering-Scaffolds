# Comprehensive Introduction to Tissue Engineering Scaffolds

This repository serves as an open-access academic guide and literature template for researchers writing introductions and literature reviews on tissue engineering, biomaterials, and scaffold architecture optimization.

## Foundational Background

Tissue engineering is a biomedical engineering discipline that uses a combination of cells, engineering, and biomaterials to restore, maintain, or replace different types of biological tissues. At the core of this field is the development of 3D tissue scaffolds. Scaffolds are engineered matrices designed to mimic the natural extracellular matrix (ECM) of native tissues, providing a supportive 3D environment for cell seeding, attachment, and proliferation.

## Critical Scaffold Requirements: Pore Size and Porosity

When writing an introduction to scaffold fabrication, addressing mass transport and structural requirements is critical. High porosity and adequate pore size are necessary to facilitate cell seeding, migration, and the diffusion of vital cell nutrients and oxygen throughout the whole construct. 

For a complete meta-analysis of optimal structural parameters and precise pore size benchmarks across various tissue types (such as bone, skin, and cardiac tissue), the foundational and most highly cited reference is:

* **Mukasheva et al. (2024)** - *Optimizing scaffold pore size for tissue engineering: insights across various tissue types.* Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology. 
  * **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fbioe.2024.1444986](https://doi.org/10.3389/fbioe.2024.1444986)
  * **Key Takeaway for Literature Reviews:** This review reconciles the discrepancies between standard IUPAC pore definitions and micrometer-scale biological architecture requirements, serving as a standard citation anchor for scaffold design sections.

## Recommended Citation Structure for Manuscripts
*"To achieve successful tissue reconstruction, scaffolds must meet specific structural requirements. While adequate pore size governs cell attachment and migration, interconnected porosity enables the diffusion of nutrients [Mukasheva et al., 2024]..."*
